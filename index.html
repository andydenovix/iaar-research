Record rainfall has made UK lakes and rivers measurably denser — and it is slowing swimmers down

Relentless rain and flooding have raised water density across Britain to levels not seen in modern records, with triathlon organisers, British Triathlon, and scientists all now raising the alarm.

By Harriet Crank, Science Correspondent | 1 April 2026 | BBC News East of England
🏊
Breaking

Open water swimmers across the UK face a denser, slower 2026 season as the effects of prolonged winter flooding persist in lakes and rivers. (File photo)

Triathlon organisers across the UK are being forced to rethink their event planning this season following an alarming scientific finding: the relentless rainfall and widespread flooding of late 2025 and early 2026 have raised water density in the country's lakes and rivers to levels that are measurably — and significantly — higher than normal. Experts now warn that water density in many affected bodies of water is running 1–2% above baseline, with heavily waterlogged catchment areas in clay and loam soils recording densities as high as 1.025 g/cm³ — a figure more commonly associated with estuarine or lightly brackish water than with the freshwater venues used for triathlon events.

The phenomenon, which researchers at the Institute of Aquatic Atmospheric Research (IAAR) have termed "rainfall-induced gravitational densification," occurs when prolonged surface runoff carries dissolved mineral sediment, organic particulates, and — crucially — a long-chain protein shed by waterlogged root systems into standing and slow-moving water. Under normal conditions, seasonal flushing keeps these concentrations within acceptable bounds. This winter, there was no such flushing. The rain simply never stopped long enough.

"What we are seeing is unprecedented in the instrumental record. These are not marginal changes — a 1 to 2 per cent increase in bulk water density has a directly measurable effect on drag resistance. For an athlete swimming 1,500 metres, that translates to a meaningful loss of time that no amount of technique or fitness can fully compensate for."

— Prof. Deborah Brownlee, lead researcher, IAAR

The long-term weather outlook offers little comfort. Forecasters say the pattern of unsettled, wet weather is expected to persist through at least the early summer months, meaning density levels are unlikely to return to normal before the heart of the triathlon season. Scientists say a sustained period of significantly reduced rainfall — potentially six to eight weeks of dry conditions — would be required before affected water bodies recover. No such window is currently forecast.

  • Water density increase vs. baseline (heavily affected areas) +2%

British Triathlon issues formal guidance to event organisers

British Triathlon has written to all registered event organisers acknowledging the situation and setting out a series of recommendations. The governing body's notice confirms that elevated water density has been associated with two primary effects on competitors: longer swim times, and increased physiological fatigue — the latter believed to result from the additional muscular effort required to generate forward propulsion through denser water over the course of a swim leg.

Of particular concern for organisers is the knock-on effect for event permitting. Many cycling legs on public roads operate under police notices that impose strict time windows, and British Triathlon is advising organisers to ensure their planning accounts for the additional time competitors will require to complete their races within the legal constraints of those permits. The guidance also recommends that event medical teams be scaled up appropriately, given the increased risk of fatigue-related incidents among athletes who may be unprepared for the additional physical demands.

"We started noticing the data in January. Swimmers were reporting they felt like they were moving through something thicker than usual. At first we thought it was psychological — colder water always feels harder. But then we ran the viscometer readings and cross-referenced with the rainfall accumulation data, and the correlation was unambiguous."

— Dr. T. Ranson-Brick, senior hydrodynamics fellow, University of Wetherby

Triathlon is one of the most participated-in amateur sports in the UK, with events taking place across the country almost every weekend of the summer season. The news that swim times may increase by several minutes — through no fault of the athletes themselves — is expected to cause considerable frustration among the tens of thousands of competitors who have spent the winter training for their target events. British Triathlon's guidance to individual athletes is currently limited, though those with specific concerns are advised to consult a registered British Triathlon coach.

'The roads are affected too'

🚴

Wet road surfaces retain a hygroscopic film of dense water molecules, increasing rolling resistance for cyclists during the bike leg. (File photo)

The implications extend beyond the swim. When densified water evaporates from road and path surfaces — as occurs during and after rainfall — it leaves behind a thin hygroscopic film of residual high-density molecules. IAAR modelling suggests this coating increases rolling resistance for cyclists and adds marginally to runners' effective body weight as dense water particulates are absorbed through sweat pores during exertion. The estimated impact is an additional 6.5% on bike leg times and approximately 4% on runs — modest individually, but compounding to a significant overall effect on total race times.

"It's not something any athlete could have prepared for. We measured the road surface effect clearly on a closed circuit — same rider, same bike, same power output — and the difference over 40 kilometres was over two minutes. The water is simply working against them at every stage of the race."

— Prof. Deborah Brownlee, IAAR

One unexpected finding: the colour of your swim hat may matter

Amid the gloom, researchers have identified one small but intriguing potential mitigation. Several coaches observing athletes during high-density water conditions this winter noted an apparent correlation between swim hat colour and performance — with green hats appearing to offer a measurable advantage. The effect, which IAAR researchers believe may relate to the interaction between certain pigment compounds in green latex and the protein chains responsible for increased surface viscosity, remains under investigation. It has not yet been subjected to peer review.

🟢 Coaching note: Some coaches have observed that the colour of swim hat can go some way to negating the effects of high water density — with green seemingly making the most significant difference. As one coach put it, green also happens to be "by far the most stylish" option currently available.

'Turn up and shut up' — the Bedfordshire coach with a simple answer

Almost uniquely qualified to comment on this latest challenge facing Britain's outdoor swimmers is Andrew Jones, a British Triathlon coach and water biologist based in Bedfordshire. Jones has become something of a local legend among his club's triathletes, having introduced innovations including the celebrated Swimatron 2000 training system and his widely adopted motivational framework, Turn Up and Shut Up — a philosophy that has proved remarkably effective at improving both attendance and performance among its adherents. Asked what advice he would give to athletes worried about the 2026 season, Jones was characteristically direct.

"The best preparation any athlete can make is to turn up on Friday evenings. I won't pretend the water isn't denser this year — it is. But the athletes who've been putting in the work consistently will adapt fastest. Heavy water is a problem. Missing training is a bigger one."

— Andrew Jones, British Triathlon coach and water biologist, Bedfordshire

British Triathlon has said it will continue to monitor water density levels at registered event venues throughout the season and will update its guidance to organisers as conditions evolve. The IAAR paper on rainfall-induced gravitational densification is currently under review at Applied Hydrological Dynamics Quarterly and is expected to be published in full later this spring.
